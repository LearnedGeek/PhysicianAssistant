## Key Finding: No Double Taxation Treaty Exists Between Peru and US

Peru and the United States do **NOT** have a Convention to Avoid Double Taxation (CDI). The tax relationship is governed by each country's domestic law plus a limited information-sharing agreement.

This is significant for the DrOk business structure.

---

## How It Works Without a Treaty

### 1. Tax Credit Mechanism (Unilateral)

- **Article 88(e) of Peru's Income Tax Law (LIR):** Peruvian tax residents can deduct taxes paid abroad on foreign-source income
- **Limit:** Credit cannot exceed the "average rate" applied to foreign income in Peru. Excess US taxes paid above this limit are **not recoverable and cannot be carried forward**
- **Documentation:** Must have IRS certificate of payment/withholding to claim credit with SUNAT (Peru's tax authority)
- **Reference:** SUNAT Informe 024-2017

### 2. Information Exchange Agreement

- **Decreto Ley 25665:** Peru and US share tax information for administration and fraud prevention
- This is a **transparency tool**, not a tax benefit — no reduced withholding rates
- SUNAT and IRS can share information about taxpayers in either country

### 3. FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act)

- Peru has **NOT signed a formal IGA** (Intergovernmental Agreement) with the US for FATCA
- However, Peru's financial system and SUNAT have adapted regulations for **Automatic Exchange of Financial Information** (D.S. 256-2018-EF)
- Peruvian banks report account balances and returns of non-residents following international standards

### 4. TLC (Free Trade Agreement) — Does NOT Help

- The Peru-US TLC (2009) is NOT a tax treaty
- Chapter 10 (Investment) protects against expropriation but **reserves tax authority to each country's domestic law**
- The TLC does not reduce Income Tax withholding rates

## Practical Impact for DrOk

| Scenario | Tax Implication |
|---|---|
| **Mark receives revenue from Peru** (platform fees) | Peru may withhold tax as foreign-source income. Mark claims credit on US return, limited to average rate. |
| **Peruvian JV entity pays dividends to Mark** | Standard 30% US federal withholding on foreign dividends (no treaty reduction). Credit against Peruvian tax limited. |
| **Learned Geek (US LLC) receives license fees from DrOk (Peruvian entity)** | Peru withholding on payments to non-domiciled entities. Mark credits against US tax. |
| **Mark holds equity in a Peruvian entity** | FBAR and Form 8938 reporting obligations likely triggered. Possible PFIC implications. |

### Recommendation

The **two-entity model** (Learned Geek US LLC receives platform license fees from DrOk) is the cleanest tax structure because:
- Revenue flows through a US entity first — avoids the worst of the 30% withholding trap
- Platform license fees are a business expense for DrOk, reducing its Peruvian tax base
- Mark's US tax obligations are straightforward — income from a US LLC
- Equity in the JV is a smaller component, reducing FBAR/8938 complexity

**Mark should consult a US tax accountant specializing in international income before finalizing the JV structure.**
